spelling reform

This spelling reform doesn't make all of English spelling phonetic. There are actually a bunch of flaws with doing that. Chiefly, it would make it a lot harder to recognise prefixes and suffixes: compare column and columnal: in the second, the 'n' is actually pronounced, and it makes the word much more regular than it sounds. Also, if we were to spell phonetically in entirety, it would end up having to accomodate specific speech patterns. Would other dialects have significantly different spellings? Another obvious problem is how used to people are with the current system, making adjustment difficult, for signage must be changed. Then, children will find it difficult to have to learn two different systems: one used before and the other now.

It's as a result of this that I present the all-new Patellian spelling reform for the English language.

It's familiar, yet it gives an idea of the phonetic makeup of a word, while retaining obvious polymorphological structures of words.

Here are the changes I shall enact:

  1. Þe new letter þōrn (Þþ) will bē added to replāċĕ 'th'.
  2. Long vowels will now havĕ macrons (līnes) ōver þem.
  3. Any 'c' or 'g' māking a soft sound will have dots over them (including ch->ċh). ĊċĠġ
  4. 'Ph' shall now alwāys bē replāċed with 'f'.
  5. Put a brēvĕ acċent on sīlent letters.
  6. Removĕ acċents on foreiǧn words.

This system maintains optimum readability and maximum simplicity of morpheme recognition while making it easy to use and learn for new users. Or, in oþer words:
